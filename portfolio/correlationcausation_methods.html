teaching why "correlation doesn't imply causation"
testing best methods of instruction

Overview of the Problem: Psychology students - and the general public - can typically parrot the phrase, "correlation doesn't imply causation". Even still, people tend to make causal claims about correlational findings. And, having taught an undergraduate research methods in psychology course for multiple years, I learned that students struggle to develop a deeper understanding of this concept.

Solution: We developed an intervention that uses causal diagrams to teach undergraduate students why they should make stronger causal claims based on the findings from an experiment versus an observational study. We also tested three different methods of instruction for the intervention, to find which methods work best for Intro Psych students and Research Methods students.

Participants: We recruited 263 Intro Psych students and 336 Research Methods students at the University of Pittsburgh. Students in the Research Methods course were farther along in the psychology program, and therefore had more background knowledge. The entire study, including the intervention & measures, was completed on Qualtrics.

Using Causal Diagrams to Improve Understanding
We taught every participant how to use causal diagrams to illustrate alternative explanations for a correlation. Our goal was for participants to learn that there are more explanations for a correlation in an observational study than in an experiment. If they could learn this, they may be less likely to make erroneous causal claims about observational studies.

For example, consider a study that finds a positive correlation between meditation and life satisfaction.

If the study is purely observational/correlational, it is entirely possible that meditating causes people to have higher life satisfaction (and vice versa).

One explanation for a correlation in a hypothetical observational study.

On the other hand, there are two alternative possibilities:

  • Maybe, it's a case of reverse causality, and people who have higher life satisfaction are more likely to seek out self-care activities like meditating.
  • Maybe, it's a case of a confound/common cause, and the two variables aren't causally related; instead, a third variable causes both. For example, people who are stressed at work may have less time to meditate and also have lower life satisfaction. It would look like life satisfaction and meditation are correlated, but it's really that work stress causes both variables.

Alternative explanations for a correlation in a hypothetical observational study.

Why does this matter? In an experiment, we can rule out alternative possibilities for a correlation because of random assignment to conditions. Therefore, we can be more confident in making causal claims about experiments. We should be very hesitant to (read: almost never!) make causal claims about observational studies.
A/B Testing: Three Different Interventions

After learning about causal diagrams, participants completed two practice problems that applied the concepts from the tutorial. The goal was for participants to think about whether each causal diagram was possible in a hypothetical observational study or experiment.

Participants were assigned to one of three interventions; the format of the two practice problems differed across the interventions. We modeled the interventions based on prior research that shows different instructional methods can promote different features of knowledge.

The Three Interventions:
  1. Worked Example: Participants were given the practice problem and the solution simultaneously. Prior research suggests it can help with learning about procedural steps in problem-solving, but not necessarily with reducing errors in understanding.
  2. Analogical Comparison: Participants made comparisons between the practice problem and a previous example in the tutorial. Prior research suggests it can help make connections between conceptual knowledge and procedural steps, but not necessarily reduce errors in misunderstanding.
  3. Self Explanation: Participants self-generated their own causal diagrams and reasoning for whether alternative explanations were possible. Prior research suggests it can help with applying concepts to abstract principles, but not necessarily learning procedural steps.
Measuring Correlation-Causation Discrimination

At pretest and posttest, we asked participants to read and answer questions about vignettes that described a hypothetical study in which the researchers found a correlation. The design of the study was either an observational study or an experiment.

We hypothesized that before the intervention, participants would say that a correlation supports a causal claim for both observational studies and experiments. After the intervention, they should make stronger claims for experiments than for observational studies.

Example Task: Observational Study Vignette

“Researchers at the Sleep Research Society have found that people who are tired spend more money on food purchases. Participants reported the average number of hours that they slept each night and then submitted their grocery and restaurant bills to the researchers at the end of every week for analysis. Participants who reported sleeping less than five hours a night spent more money on food. So, it follows that getting more sleep will reduce the amount of money someone spends on food.”
  1. To what extent do you think that the study findings support the journalist’s conclusion that getting more sleep will reduce the amount of money someone spends on food?
  2. Do you think that the study is an experiment/randomized controlled trial or an observational/correlational study?

Example of an observational study vignette & questions.

Qualitative Measurement of Understanding At pretest, we also asked participants to explain "What does the phrase ‘Correlation does not equal/imply causation’ mean to you?”. After reading a subset of participants' responses, a group of research assistants and I categorized their responses as "stronger" or "weaker" explanations. If the participant mentioned the need for random assignment/an experiment to make a causal claim, or the possibility of alternative explanations for a correlation, their response was coded as a stronger explanation.
Participants' Knowledge before the Intervention

Participants made more causal judgments for experiments than observational studies at pretest. This was unexpected (!) - we thought judgments would be more causal for observational studies before the intervention.

It was particularly surprising that participants had some correlation-causation discrimination at pretest, because most were unable to explain why correlation doesn't imply causation. Less than 18% of Research Methods students and 15% of Intro Psych students gave a strong explanation of "why correlation doesn't equal/imply causation" at pretest.

This suggests at least two things - 1) there may be a disconnect between genuine understanding and application of the principle; 2) there is room for improvement in participants' understanding.

Effectiveness of the Interventions

Participants learned about correlation-causation discrimination during the interventions! At posttest, judgments became more causal when they thought the study was an experiment and less causal when they thought it was an observational study.

However, when we analyzed their judgments based on the actual design of the study (and not their beliefs, which could be inaccurate), we didn't find learning. This means that participants' understanding was incomplete; improvement was impeded by an inability to accurately distinguish between observational studies and experiments.

Correlation-Causation Discrimination by Believed Study Design.

Were there differences between the interventions? Yes, but it depended on the sample! At posttest, the Self Explanation condition showed the most improvement in the Intro Psych sample but the least improvement (they actually did worse!) in the Research Methods sample.
In general, did participants get better at making judgments that are appropriate for different study designs? Yes, but we think that they could be better. At posttest, the difference in judgments for observational studies versus experiments is significant, but not very big; ideally, judgments would be even less causal for observational studies and more causal for experiments.

Future Directions / Takeaways
  • In the next study, we thought about how to improve the tutorial to increase correlation-causation discrimination at posttest. First, it looks like participants need more instruction about how to tell if a design is an observational study or an experiment. Second, we changed the wording or presentation of items or content in the tutorial to emphasize the important aspects of the tutorial.
  • We also wondered whether this type of intervention could improve performance on related tasks, like knowing how to design an observational study versus an experiment. In a follow-up study, we tested whether participants designed the correct type of study when prompted.
  • More generally, I learned a lot about mentoring & qualitative coding. In the beginning, my team could spend upwards of 15 minutes trying to resolve a disagreement about how to categorize a single response. I learned how to guide the research assistants back on track, to focus on the bigger picture and not get too bogged down in the details. At the same time, this was a learning experience for the students and they needed practice with considering alternative perspectives. Sometimes, there wasn't a perfect answer for how to code a participant's response. I learned when to let the research assistants discuss in more detail versus step in and choose the best option so that we could keep moving.
