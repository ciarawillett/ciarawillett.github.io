Overview of the Problem: Psychology students - and the general public - can typically parrot the phrase, "correlation doesn't imply causation". Even still, people tend to make causal claims about correlational findings. And, having taught an undergraduate research methods in psychology course for multiple years, I learned that students struggle to develop a deeper understanding of this concept.

Solution: We developed an intervention that uses causal diagrams to teach undergraduate students why they should make stronger causal claims based on the findings from an experiment versus an observational study. We also tested three different methods of instruction for the intervention, to find which methods work best for Intro Psych students and Research Methods students.

Using Causal Diagrams to Improve Understanding

We taught every participant how to use causal diagrams to illustrate alternative explanations for a correlation. Our goal was to show that in an observational study, there are more potential explanations for a correlation than in an experiment.

For example, consider a study that finds a positive correlation between meditation and life satisfaction. If the study is purely observational/correlational, it is possible that meditating causes people to have higher life satisfaction (and vice versa). On the other hand, there are two alternative possibilities:

  • Maybe, it's a case of reverse causality, and people who have higher life satisfaction are more likely to seek out self-care activities like meditating.
  • Maybe, it's a case of a third variable/common cause, and the two variables aren't causally related; instead, a third variable causes both. For example, people who are stressed at work may have less time to meditate and also have lower life satisfaction. It would look like life satisfaction amd meditation are correlated, but it's really that work stress causes both variables.

Why does this matter? In an experiment, we can rule out these alternative possibilities because of random assignment to conditions. Therefore, there are more explanations for a correlation in an observational study. We should be more careful about making causal claims in an observational study, whereas we can be more confident when making causal claims in an experiment.

Testing Different Interventions

After learning about causal diagrams, participants completed two practice problems that applied the concepts from the tutorial to a hypothetical observational study and a hypothetical experiment. The goal was for participants to think about whether each causal diagram was possible in the hypothetical scenario. We thought that if they learn there are more explanations for a correlation in an observational study, they may be less likely to erroneously make causal claims about observational studies.

Next, I selected three of the Crazy 8 ideas (one low, one medium, one high risk) and created storyboards for each one. I conducted brief speed dating sessions to get feedback from target customers:

  • Participants reiterated the findings from the discovery process: they value insights from verified customers.
  • This insight does not necessarily have to come from a trusted family member or friend - having access to feedback in general is useful in making the decision about a pricing plan.

Storyboards created for testing in speed dating rounds.

Using this feedback, I designed an interactive prototype on Figma that included two features:

  • A clearly formatted table that illustrated the differences between the three plans. Each column highlighted the corresponding feature for that plan so that users could easily see the differences.
  • Add a review page for each of the plans so that users can get feedback from verified customers. Previous customers can leave a detailed description of their experience, rate how much they like the plan (on a scale of 1 to 5). I also included a feature for users to rate how helpful they found the feedback, to make high quality reviews more convenient.

(1) Table to show differences between plans; (2) Reviews from former customers.

I conducted a series of think-aloud interviews to conduct usability testing for the prototype. When developing the tasks to assess usability, I was primarily concerned about:

  • Does the participant trust the reviews?
  • Is there information missing from the product comparison page?
  • What does the user find confusing or difficult to accomplish?

Participants found the table helpful and provided the necessary information to compare plans and understand key differences between them, however they still did not trust the customer reviews! One reason for this is because the reviews were automatically sorted by "high" to "low" - none of the participants thought to change how the reviews were sorted, and so they thought that Fios was "curating" the reviews to only include positive feedback.

Future Directions / Takeaways
  • The next step would be to conduct A/B testing to test whether adding user reviews actually improves customer trust and conversion.
  • A key takeaway was that customers from a wide age range purchase internet or change internet service providers (ISPs) and their needs differ. I noticed a trend where older participants did not care about the reviews and said that the reviews actually reduced their trust in the ISP; whereas the younger participants sought out this information when making their purchase. If this trend is reliable, future solutions would need to balance the needs of both users.
