Discovery Process

I began with a series of contextual interviews on Zoom to understand 1) the needs of the customer, 2) the wants of the customer, and 3) what informs their decision process. I simultaneously incorporated some competitive analysis by having participants walk through the process of purchasing internet from Fios and a competitor, Xfinity.

I used affinity diagramming to analyze the data and identified two key problem areas:

  • Participants did not understand the differences between the pricing plans offered by Fios. They felt frustration about not knowing which option to choose.
  • There was a substantial level of distrust expressed towards internet service providers and what they wrote on their website. Primarily, they were worried about hidden costs.

Example Insight from Affinity Diagram: Participants tended to distrust ISPs and desired more information about the differences between pricing plans.

Exploratory Process: Design Sprints & Speed Dating

I started with the design sprint method, Crazy 8's, to sketch low and high risk solutions. During the interviews, participants said they would rely on family and friends for advice about selecting a plan or provider. To integrate information about the different plans & feedback from actual customers, I wanted to know if the customer would value other sources of feedback (e.g., a collated review section on the website).

Next, I selected three of the ideas and created storyboards for each one. I conducted brief speed dating sessions to get feedback from target customers:

  • Participants reiterated the findings from the discovery process: they value insights from verified customers.
  • The feedback does not necessarily have to come from a trusted family member or friend - having access to feedback in general is useful in making the decision about a pricing plan.

Crafting a Solution: Speed-Dating & Storyboards
I used the design sprint method "crazy 8's" to draft potential solutions to key issues identified during contextual inquiry.

Next, I drafted storyboards to model low- and high-risk adaptations of the crazy-8's. Findings from speed dating sessions reiterated that users want feedback about the different plans from existing customers, and revealed that a stranger's feedback was as valuable as a friend or family member's.

Testing: Prototyping and User Think-Alouds
I created an interactive prototype that modeled two new functionalities. First, a customer feedback page that customers could search before choosing a plan. Second, a simple comparison table to highlight the differences between each pricing plan.
Insights from think-aloud testing suggest that the comparison table was effective, and required some minor tweaking. However, the customer feedback page did not adequately resolve feelings of distrust; potential solutions are listed below.
Future Considerations / Possibilities
If I were to continue this project, here's some ways I would have addressed user feedback:
  • Further highlight plan differences by reducing repetitive text in the comparison table.
  • Improve the trustworthiness & authenticity of user reviews by 1) adding a "verified customer" badge and 2) use actual (not hypothetical) customer feedback quotes.
  • Adopt a star-based review system to mirror familiar rating systems, as opposed to creating a novel system where ratings were represented with the Verizon check.
