fostering resilience and engagement with law students
at the University of Pittsburgh

Overview of the Problem: Although first-year law students begin their studies full of enthusiasm, many are full of anxiety by the end of the first-semester. They have anxieties about grades & performance in the classroom, future job prospects, the competitive atmosphere. Many experience imposter syndrome and feel that they do not belong; others experience a similar feeling called "pluralistic ignorance" - the idea that no one else around them was struggling. Often, students adopt a "fixed mindset" - that one has a fixed amount of intelligence, and that they are unable to grow or change.

Solution: The Fostering Resilience and Engagement project is a collaborative effort by law professors and psychologists to address the social and academic needs of students; to help students become more resilient and the process of legal education more humane. Our team is testing a series of well-timed interventions during the students' first year of law school. These interventions encourage the student to adopt an "adaptive mindset", with the goal of promoting resilience & growth.

Collaborators: Ann Sinsheimer, PhD, JD, University of Pittsburgh School of Law; Omid Fotuhi, PhD, Learning, Research, & Development Center.

Focus Groups / The Belongingness Intervention

The goal of the Belongingness Intervention is to encourage law students to adopt an "adaptive mindset" - that they can grow their abilities and that everyone has the chance to succeed. We implemented two versions (in-person and online) of the intervention to test if different methods wree more effective. The in-person intervention was completed during students' first-year writing seminar, and was held in a focus-group style format over Zoom during the pandemic.

In Person / Zoom Intervention
  • Discussion facilitator frames students as the foremost experts of the law student experience & their input is valued and will be used to help future students. This was established at the very beginning - it is both true and also a strategy to encourage sharing through "helping others".
  • Short ice breaker to create an environment where students feel comfortable sharing.
  • Write down 3 bad experiences and 3 good experiences from their first year.
  • Encourage students to share more about their experiences.
  • At key time points in the intervention, the facilitator explained the concept of "pluralistic ignorance" and encouraged students to reframe their perspectives. For example, is there a right profile for a law student?

Online Intervention
  • Completed individually on Qualtrics.
  • Student reads a series of anonymized one-paragraph essays from former 1L students about their experiences. The selected essays highlighted common themes about pluralistic ignorance & feelings that they didn't belong. Importantly, the essays conveyed the message of reframing ones mindset, and that over time, one comes to feel that they belong at Pitt Law; that they can be successful.
  • The student also writes their own summary of their experiences during the first year of law school.

Data Analysis

At the end of the survey, 144 participants responded to an open-ended question in which they could reflect on their experience with the grading curve at Pitt Law.

I categorized participants' responses into several main themes that best represented students' experiences, and compiled a comprehensive report to summarize the data. In general, most (74%) students were not in favor of the curve.

When drafting a report for the administration, the primary goal was to ensure that students' experiences were communicated. In addition to providing aggregate data about the main themes in participants' responses, I also included individual quotes - because students would not be at the meeting, I wanted to highlight their voices in the document. If there were different opinions about an issue, I sampled a proportionate number of quotations from each side.

What We Learned

The students overwhelmingly expressed negative feelings and experiences about the grading curve. A few key themes emerged:
  1. Grades are not merit-based and do not reflect the individual's knowledge or actual abilities. (57% of respondents).
  2. The curve affects students' motivation (49% of respondents); roughly two-thirds of these participants said that it had a negative effect, and some said that it had a somewhat positive effect because it encouraged competition.
  3. The curve had a negative impact on mental health and caused a significant amount of stress for 41% of respondents.
  4. Students (22%) said that the curve negatively affects their interpersonal relationships or creates an environment that is too competitive.
  5. Several students (14%) said that when given the choice, they do not select classes that use a curve.
What Happened Next & Placing our Findings into Context
  • After reading and discussing the findings in our report, the faculty & administration voted to remove the grading curve policy.
  • The Fostering Resilience and Engagement project has been working on a series of interventions that encourage students to adopt an adaptive mindset - that their intelligence can grow and they can overcome challenges during law school. However, it is difficult to encourage an adaptive mindset at the individual level when institutional policies - like a grading curve - communicate a fixed mindset. Our hope is that the adoption of an adaptive mindset at multiple levels of the school - the student, the faculty, the institution level - will help students thrive.
