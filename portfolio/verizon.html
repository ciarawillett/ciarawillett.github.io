class project: Fios Internet
This case study was my independent project submitted for a 'HCI for Tech Executives' class at Carnegie Mellon University. I used several UX research methods to improve the experience of purchasing internet from the Verizon Fios website.

Project Dates: October to December 2020

Research Methods: stakeholder mapping, contextual inquiry, affinity clustering, journey mapping, storyboards, speed dating, prototyping, think-aloud usability testing.

Tools Used: Figma, Miro.

Key Problems: Distrust in ISP's, Uncertainty in Pricing Plans
In a series of contextual inquiries, participants walked through the process of purchasing internet from Fios and a competitor.

During the exercise, participants expressed a substantial level of distrust towards internet service providers and had concerns about hidden costs. Additionally, participants felt they were unable to adequately compare pricing plans.

Example Insight from Affinity Diagram: Participants tended to distrust ISPs and desired more information about pricing plans.

Crafting a Solution: Speed-Dating & Storyboards
I used the design sprint method "crazy 8's" to draft potential solutions to key issues identified during contextual inquiry.

Next, I drafted storyboards to model a few low-risk and high-risk adaptations for some of the crazy-8's. Findings from speed dating sessions reinforced that participants desired insight about the different plans from existing customers, and revealed that they valued opinions about ISPs from strangers as much as friends & family members.

Testing: Prototyping and User Think-Alouds
I created an interactive prototype that modeled two new functions during the internet purchasing process.

First, I addressed the problem of distrust in ISP's by testing a customer feedback page that customers could search before choosing a plan. Second, I added a simple comparison table to highlight the differences between each pricing plan.

Insights from think-aloud testing suggest that the comparison table was effective, and required some minor tweaking. However, the customer feedback page did not adequately resolve feelings of distrust; potential solutions are listed below.

Future Considerations / Possibilities
If I were to continue this project, here's some ways I would have addressed user feedback:
  • Further highlight plan differences by reducing repetitive text in the comparison table.
  • Improve the trustworthiness/authenticity of user reviews by 1) adding a "verified customer" badge and 2) use actual (not hypothetical) customer feedback quotes.
  • Adopt a star-based review system to mirror familiar rating systems, as opposed to creating a novel system where ratings were represented with the Verizon check.
